Postdoctoral Researcher - 08/2023 - Present

 

Supervisor: Dr. Monika Soraisam

NOIRLab’s Gemini North Observatory, 670 N Aohoku Place, Hilo, HI, USA, 96720

 

My research centers on mining Hubble Space Telescope (HST) data from the Panchromatic Hubble Andromeda Treasury (PHAT) survey to identify and characterize variable stars in M31. I use a combination of light curve analysis and difference imaging to identify and verify variable stars across survey images. The first study in this project is now published, focusing on variable stars within the highly crowded stellar clusters of M31. For the first time, we combined the isochrone-derived stellar initial mass and evolutionary phase of each variable star with its observed variability properties to identify the most likely type of variable star for each source. A total of 86 cluster variable stars were confirmed and classified, including RV Tauri variables, red supergiants, Î± Cygni variables and a possible super-asymptotic giant star. I am now investigating Mira variables in the PHAT survey, exploring how the longer baseline of ZTF observations can be used to obtain variability periods. This will allow for the period-luminosity relations of Mira variables in M31 to be investigated, potentially allowing this population to be used to estimate cosmic distances.

 

Doctor of Philosophy in Astrophysics - 09/2017 - 07/2013

 

Supervisor: Professor Alan Fitzsimmons

Thesis Title: “Characterising Transneptunian Binaries in the Hubble Archive using Markov Chain Monte Carlo Methods”

Queen’s University Belfast, Belfast, Northern Ireland, United Kingdom, BT7 1NN

 

My PhD focused on measuring optical and near-infrared surface properties of trans-Neptunian Binaries (TNBs). I devel oped and applied a custom-made Markov Chain Monte Carlo PSF-fitting algorithm to archival HST data taken with Wide Field Camera 3, obtaining binary component surface colours, their short- and long-term variability, relative reflectance and partial light curves. I found the known correlation in TNB component optical colours appears to be dependent on ob ject dynamical class, with evidence for similar correlations in near-infrared wavelengths, implying the components of each binary have similar compositions. I also identified TNBs with changes in relative reflectance both between components and over time, indicating different compositions and changes in composition with rotation, respectively. Publication of the results of this study is expected in early 2025.

 

MPhys Astrophysics, First Class Honours - 09/2012 - 07/2016

 

Supervisor: Dr. Richard Parker

Project: “The Effect of Substructure in Star Forming Regions on the Occurrence of the Kozai Mechanism in Binary Star Systems”

University of Liverpool, Foundation Building, Brownlow Hill, Liverpool, L69 7ZX

 

I examined the effects of different virial ratios in star forming regions on binary stars and their satellites. I analysed two fractal simulations of star forming regions evolving over 10Myrs, finding a greater number of preserved binary stars within the Kozai angle range in the lower virial ratio (highly substructured) simulation, with higher mass primary stars also being more likely to experience the Kozai Mechanism.
